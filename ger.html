As Europe's largest economy and second most populous nation (after Russia), Germany is a key member of the continent's economic, political, and defense organizations, Germany has expended considerable funds to bring eastern productivity and wages up to western standards. The German economy - the fifth largest economy in the world in PPP terms and Europe's largest - is a leading exporter of machinery, vehicles, chemicals, and household equipment. Germany benefits from a highly skilled labor force, but, like its Western European neighbors, faces significant demographic challenges to sustained long-term growth. Low fertility rates and a large increase in net immigration are increasing pressure on the country's social welfare system and necessitate structural reforms


Introduction to Immigration Law

Germany has visa category based system; the grant of residence permit for the purpose of employment is subject to meeting the minimum specific requirements of the visa category. Applicants must check whether the conditions for the grant of the permit to the individual in question can be met. This includes both general conditions that are the prerequisite for the grant of permit [Ex: sufficient health insurance] as well as specific conditions that are particular to each visa category [Ex: local employment contract for some visa categories or business case for self-employment visa categories].

The number of visas that can be granted under each of visa categories is unlimited as long as the labour authorities don’t come to conclusion that the position can be filled from the local job market in the case of visa categories with a labour market test requirement.


Immigration Authorities:

Depending on their nationality and the purpose and length of their stay, foreign nationals may either require an entry clearance in the form of a visa or may enter Germany without a visa and apply for a residence permit within Germany
The main immigration authorities concerned with the applications for visas and work permits respectively are Germany Embassies or Consulate aboard and the local foreign offices in Germany, the local labor offices and the centre for Recruitment of foreign and Expert staff (ZAV)
Certain nationalities within Europe benefit from the EU Waiver programme and don’t need work permit to enter, others have to obtain visa before entering Germany. The responsible diplomatic representation aboard or the local foreign office is solely responsible for the applicant, although it will liaise with local labour offices, through ZAV.
Following the most recent major reform of the German immigration system, which came into effect on 1st January 2005, residence and work permits are granted in the form of residence permit for the purpose of employment, which is granted by the foreign office after the local labor office, through the ZAV has given its consent to the grant, if required to do so. This avoids the previous process of applicant to deal with two different authorities or to file two separate applications.
